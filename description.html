Description

Matching games are games that require players to match similar elements. As the name implies, participants need to find a match for a word, picture, or card. For example, students place 12 picture of cards; composed of 6 pairs, face down in random order. A player turns over two cards at a time, with the goal of turning over a matching pair, by using their memory. This is also known as the Pelmanism principle, after Christopher Louis Pelman, a British psychologist of the first half of the 20th century.

Mostly younger players are often able to compete with older players because they can be very good at memory-based games.
  1. The objective is to collect the 6 pairs of cards.
  2. The game automatically shuffle the cards and face down, in rows with the name "NIPTICT".
  3. Play then continues in a clockwise direction(Optional).
  4. The trick is to remember which cards are where.
  5. A player turns over any two cards (one at a time) and the computer will check them if the cards match
  6. When a player turns over two cards that do not match, those cards are turned face down again automatically(in the same position).
  7. If a player successfully match a pair they will receive alert of message that provided by game to show how many flips that the player has been flipped.
